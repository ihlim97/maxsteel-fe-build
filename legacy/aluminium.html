Aluminium

Background

Aluminum is widely used for its low density, high electrical and thermal conductivities, good strength as well as its corrosion resistivity from passivation. Aluminum is a non ferrous metal and is usually produced as an alloy for better mechanical properties.

Aluminum alloys are very malleable with high ductility even at very low temperatures due to the crystal structure. Its key limitation would be the low melting point of about 660 °C.

Aluminum is produced from Bauxite which goes through a series of refinement into Alumina, smelted electrolytically in Aluminum, followed by either rolling, extrusion or casting into various types of fabricated products.

Mechanical properties can also be enhanced by cold working, solution strengthening and aging. Composition of aluminum alloys would be designated by a four-digit number that shows the main impurity contents or levels. A temper designation would follow after this number to indicate the type or level of treatment the alloy had been subjected to. Detailed information can be found in the PDF guide.

Characteristics

The general appearance of Aluminum is silvery or dull grey, depending on the type of surface finish. Aluminum can be alloyed with different elements that include Copper, Manganese, Silicon, Zinc or others. This gives a series of Aluminum from the 1000 to 9000 series. Common attributes include lightweight, good corrosion resistance, ductility, electrical and thermal conductivity as well as eco-friendly, being 100% recyclable.

Applications

Aluminum is used in many applications, including automotive, computers, household appliances, food and beverage, electronics, construction, structural aerospace, semi-conductors and many others.
